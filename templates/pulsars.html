PULSARS AND MAGNETARS

Pulsating Radio Sources or Pulsars are highly magnetized rotating compact neutron stars. These stars emit electromagnetic radiation known as jets from their magnetic poles. Pulsars rotate at high speeds and appear to flicker when viewed as they are only visible when the radiation beams point towards us. The pulsating intervals range from milliseconds to seconds. Pulsars are one of the rare sources of ultra-high-energy cosmic rays. Astronomer Jocelyn Bell first observed pulsars in 1967 using a specially designed radio telescope. Pulsars helped in proving the existence of gravitational radiation. The two main sub-classes of pulsars include rotation-powered pulsars and accretion-powered pulsars. In rotation-powered pulsars, the loss of rotational energy provides power to the pulsar, whereas in accretion-powered pulsars, the gravitational potential of a pulsar is its source of power. lae and created due to a dynamic gravitational collapse, stars are the fundamental foundation blocks required for the existence of any Solar System. Life, as we know, on Earth would never be sustained had the gigantic blazing ball of hydrogen and helium, our Sun, never been formed. Stars are massive, gaseous luminous celestial spheroids composed of gases(primarily hydrogen and helium) and plasma. They radiate immense light and heat energy because of internal nuclear fusion and possess a surrounding magnetic field. Every star encompasses a strong central gravitational pull of attraction which enables the positioning of all astronomical objects in its respective solar system in stable elliptical orbits around it. Many stars also exist in pairs (binary stars), multiple systems, or star clusters.

Prominently known Pulsars:

•Vela Pulsar: The brightest known Pulsar

• PSR J0437−4715: the brightest millisecond Pulsar

• PSR J0737−3039: A double binary pulsar

• PSR B1257+12: A pulsar with planets orbiting it

• PSR J1748−2446ad: The shortest period pulsar

• AR Scorpii: The longest period pulsar

• PSR J0437−4715: Pulsar with the most stable Period

Following are the applications of Pulsars: • Maps • Pulsar Navigation • Precise Clocks • Spacetime investigation • Gravitational-wave detectors • Researching the interstellar medium

Magnetars form another class of neutron stars. These stars have strong magnetic fields measuring approximately a thousand times stronger than Earth's magnetic field. The slow and gradual decay of the magnetic field accounts for the power required by the magnetar to emit electromagnetic radiation. Magnetars are known to emit large sudden bursts of X-rays and gamma rays.

Prominently known Magnetars: • SGR 0525−66: located in the Large Magellanic Cloud* • SGR 1806−20: Sagittarius constellation • SGR 1900+14: Aquila constellation. • 1E 1048.1−5937: Carina Constellation • 1E 1048.1−5937: located in Westerlund 1*
